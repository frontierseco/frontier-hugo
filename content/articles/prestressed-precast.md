Prestressed precast concrete is one of the most efficient structural systems for mid-span commercial applications. Double-tees, hollow-core planks, inverted-tee beams, and precast columns are manufactured in a controlled plant environment, shipped to the site, and erected quickly. When it works well, it's fast and economical. When it's not coordinated properly, the rigidity of the precast system creates problems that are expensive to resolve.

## How Prestressed Precast Works

Precast elements are prestressed: high-strength strands are tensioned before the concrete is cast, then released after curing. This pre-compression allows the elements to span greater distances and carry larger loads with less depth than conventionally reinforced concrete. Double-tees can span 60 to 80 feet. Hollow-core planks can span 20 to 40 feet. These are spans that require significantly deeper sections in mild reinforced concrete or steel.

The manufacturing happens in a plant with quality control systems that field-cast concrete can't match. Consistency of concrete strength, cover, and strand placement is better in plant-cast precast than in field-poured elements. This quality advantage is one of precast's genuine strengths.

## What Has to Be Coordinated Early

Precast systems are not flexible after the fact. Once the precast producer designs and manufactures the elements, the geometry is fixed. This creates specific coordination requirements:

**Column locations must be set early.** Precast spans are determined by column locations. If the architectural plan changes after the precast layout is established, the impact can be significant: precast members don't get shortened or extended without substantial cost.

**Openings must be planned for.** Openings in hollow-core planks or double-tee stems for mechanical, electrical, and plumbing penetrations need to be located and sized before the shop drawings are complete. Field-coring precast is possible but expensive and, in some cases, structurally problematic.

**Bearing details matter.** Precast elements bear on walls, beams, or corbels at a minimum bearing length specified by the precast producer. These bearing conditions need to be detailed in the structural drawings and confirmed by the architect at the support conditions.

**Camber must be anticipated.** Prestressed elements camber upward as the strands are released. This camber varies with span, loading, and time. Floors and roofs with precast elements will not be perfectly flat. The camber differential between adjacent members needs to be accommodated in the finish floor system or grouted topping.

## When Precast Makes Sense

Prestressed precast is well-suited for:

- Parking structures, where long clear spans are architecturally desirable and the precast industry has highly developed standard sections
- Single-story retail and warehouse construction, where simple column grids and speed of construction matter
- School and institutional construction, where span requirements and durability demands align with precast's strengths

It's less suited for irregular plans, buildings with frequent penetration demands, or projects in locations where the precast producer's lead time creates a schedule problem.

## The Precast Producer as a Design Partner

Unlike most structural systems, precast elements are engineered and manufactured by the precast producer, not detailed entirely by the SE. The SE designs for the loads and specifies the minimum capacities required; the precast producer designs the specific elements to meet those requirements. The producer's shop drawings are reviewed by the SE for conformance.

This division of responsibility works well when it's understood by everyone on the team. It works poorly when the architect or contractor expects the SE to have complete element details before the producer is selected, or when the producer isn't engaged until after design development is complete.

Get the precast producer involved early. Their input on standard section availability, lead times, and plant capacity is information that affects design decisions.
