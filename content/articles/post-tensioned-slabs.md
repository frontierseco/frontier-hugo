Post-tensioned concrete is one of the most efficient structural systems available for commercial construction. The advantages are real: long spans, thin slabs, fewer columns, faster construction. But PT systems have constraints that affect architectural planning, and those constraints are much easier to work with when they're understood at the start of design rather than discovered at permit.

## How Post-Tensioning Works

In a conventional reinforced concrete slab, mild steel rebar resists tension forces after the concrete cracks. In a post-tensioned slab, high-strength steel tendons are stressed after the concrete cures, compressing the slab and counteracting applied loads before cracking occurs. The result is a slab that can span farther with less depth than conventional reinforced concrete.

For commercial floor plates, PT flat plate systems routinely achieve spans of 28 to 36 feet with slab thicknesses of 8 to 10 inches. That's structural efficiency that translates directly into reduced floor-to-floor heights, lower concrete volume, and fewer columns interrupting the floor plan.

## What Architects Need to Plan Around

**Tendon profiles are not arbitrary.** PT tendons follow a draped profile (low at midspan, high at supports) and their paths need to be coordinated with penetrations, MEP sleeves, and coring locations. You cannot core freely through a PT slab after the fact without knowing where the tendons are. This needs to be understood by the GC and any future tenant improvement contractors.

**Slab edges and re-entrant corners need attention.** PT slabs want to shorten as they're stressed. Restraint at walls, cores, and re-entrant corners can cause cracking if not detailed carefully. The structural engineer needs to know about these conditions early.

**Transfer levels require different thinking.** If columns don't stack, which is common in mixed-use buildings where parking or retail occupies lower floors, transfer beams or slabs carry significant load. Post-tensioning these members is possible, but the engineering is more complex and needs to be addressed in the structural system selection, not after the architectural program is locked.

**Penetrations must be coordinated before construction.** Slab penetrations for mechanical, electrical, plumbing, and stairs should be located and sized before the PT layout is complete. Adding penetrations during construction in a PT slab is costly and sometimes structurally significant.

## When PT Makes Sense

PT flat plate is a strong choice for:
- Office, hotel, and residential mid-rise construction with regular column grids
- Parking structures where long spans and thin slabs reduce story count
- Buildings where minimizing floor-to-floor height matters for code or massing

It's less ideal for highly irregular plans, buildings with frequent penetration demands, or projects where the contractor base lacks PT experience.

## The Early Conversation

The right time to decide whether PT is appropriate is at schematic design, when the column grid is still flexible and the floor-to-floor heights haven't been committed. An SE can quickly evaluate whether the spans and loads justify a PT system versus conventional reinforced concrete or a steel frame, and that comparison shapes everything that follows.

Post-tensioned concrete rewards early coordination. The architects and contractors who get the most out of it are the ones who treat the structural system as a design input, not a background condition.
