Mixed-use mid-rise buildings, typically retail or parking at grade with office or residential above, are among the most structurally demanding projects in commercial construction. The architectural program demands that don't stack cleanly vertically, the transfer structures required to bridge different column grids, and the competing structural requirements of different occupancies create engineering challenges that need to be addressed early and thoroughly.

## The Transfer Problem

The fundamental structural challenge in mixed-use construction is that the column grid for the upper residential or office floors and the column grid for the lower parking or retail floors are often incompatible. Residential construction favors closely spaced columns (16 to 24 feet) to support bearing walls or flat plate slabs. Parking requires longer spans (typically 60 feet or more for efficient stall layout) with minimal column interruption.

Bridging these grids requires a transfer structure at the transition level, where upper columns terminate and their loads are transferred to a smaller number of lower columns through heavy transfer beams, transfer slabs, or a combination of both.

Transfer structures carry very large loads. A transfer beam supporting four or five stories of residential construction above parking may carry thousands of kips. These are deep, heavy elements, sometimes 36 to 48 inches deep or more, that require significant floor-to-floor height at the transfer level and careful coordination with MEP systems that need to pass through or around them.

The transfer level needs to be identified and designed early. Discovering late in design that the column grids don't stack, and that a transfer structure is required, can force significant architectural changes.

## Post-Tensioned Concrete for Mixed-Use

Post-tensioned flat plate is well-suited for the residential or office levels of a mixed-use mid-rise. The combination of long spans, thin slabs, and efficient concrete use makes PT competitive with steel in this application. But PT at the transfer level requires more attention: transfer beams in PT concrete are more complex to design and more critical to construct correctly than typical PT floor slabs.

## The Podium Structure

A common mixed-use configuration is the podium building: a concrete podium (typically one to five stories) supporting a wood-frame or light-frame residential structure above. The podium provides the long spans and fire resistance required for parking and retail; the wood frame provides cost-effective construction for the residential stories.

The structural interface between the podium and the wood frame is a critical detail. The podium deck must carry the residential loads, manage moisture, provide adequate bearing for the wood framing, and accommodate differential movement between the concrete and wood systems. This detail requires careful coordination between the SE and the architect.

## Seismic and the Mixed-Use Challenge

Mixed-use buildings in seismic zones present a specific challenge: the structural systems of the upper and lower portions of the building often have different stiffnesses. A concrete podium is much stiffer than the wood frame above it. This stiffness discontinuity is a structural irregularity that requires specific analysis under ASCE 7 and can increase seismic demand on the transition zone.

The lateral system needs to be designed for the building as a whole, not treated as two separate structures. The seismic performance of the building depends on how well the transition is engineered.

## Getting the Column Grid Right

The single most important structural decision in a mixed-use mid-rise is the column grid. Every other structural decision follows from it. An SE who is involved at schematic design can evaluate whether the proposed column configuration is achievable, flag the transfer conditions, and help the architect optimize the grid to minimize cost and complexity.

Mixed-use mid-rise is a building type that benefits from early, integrated structural input more than almost any other. The teams that get this right are the ones where the SE is at the table at schematic design.
