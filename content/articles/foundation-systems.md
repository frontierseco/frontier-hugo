Foundation decisions are often made late in the design process, after architectural and structural systems are locked in. That sequence creates problems, because the right foundation type depends on soil conditions that are sometimes unknown until a geotechnical investigation is complete, and geotechnical investigations are sometimes delayed until the project feels "real enough" to spend money on.

By then, the team has been designing to an assumption. If the assumption is wrong, the downstream cost impact is significant.

## What Drives Foundation Selection

Four factors drive foundation type selection: loads, soil bearing capacity, groundwater, and differential settlement tolerance. These interact in ways that make foundation engineering more nuanced than it appears.

A building with heavy column loads on strong, uniform soils is a straightforward spread footing problem. The same building on weak, compressible soils may require driven piles or drilled piers. A building with moderate loads on expansive clays (common across the Front Range) may need a structural slab system that isolates the building from soil movement, regardless of bearing capacity.

## Spread Footings

Spread footings are the simplest and usually the least expensive foundation type when conditions allow them. They transfer column and wall loads directly to the bearing soil through a widened base. Spread footings require adequate bearing capacity at a reasonable depth and soil that is relatively uniform and not subject to significant volume change.

When soil conditions support spread footings, there's rarely a reason to use something more expensive. The challenge is confirming those conditions exist before the design is committed.

## Drilled Piers and Driven Piles

When near-surface soils are inadequate (too weak, too variable, or subject to volume change), deep foundations transfer load to competent material at depth. Drilled piers (also called drilled shafts or caissons) are bored into the soil to a specified depth and diameter, then filled with reinforced concrete. Driven piles are steel or precast concrete elements driven to refusal or to a specified capacity.

In Colorado, drilled piers are common because the depth to competent material is often manageable and local contractors have the equipment and experience. The geotechnical engineer specifies the minimum embedment depth and minimum diameter based on test borings; the SE sizes the piers for the structural loads.

## Expansive Soils: A Colorado-Specific Challenge

Expansive soils, predominantly clay-rich soils that swell when wet and shrink when dry, cause more foundation damage in Colorado than any other soil condition. The heave forces from expanding soil can exceed the weight of a light building, meaning that bearing capacity is not the governing concern. Isolation is.

Structural slab systems for expansive soils (typically a ribbed or waffle slab on grade with void forms beneath to allow soil movement) are designed to span across zones of differential movement and carry the building load independently of the soil. This approach requires coordination between the geotechnical engineer, the SE, and the architect early in design.

Ignoring expansive soils, or designing to conventional spread footings when expansive clays are present, is one of the most common and costly errors in Colorado commercial construction.

## The Geotechnical Investigation

The SE can't make good foundation recommendations without geotechnical data. A geotechnical investigation (test borings, lab testing, and a written report) provides the information needed to select and design the foundation system. Investing in a geotech report early in design is almost always worth it. The cost of the investigation is small relative to the cost of a foundation redesign discovered at permit or, worse, a foundation failure discovered after construction.

Get the soils report early. Design the foundation to match what the soils actually are.
