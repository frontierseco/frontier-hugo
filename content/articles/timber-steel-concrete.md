Architects sometimes come to an SE with a material preference already set. They want a mass timber building for its aesthetics and sustainability story, or a concrete frame for its durability, or steel because the GC they're working with knows it well. These are legitimate considerations. But the best structural system for a given project emerges from a real comparison, and that comparison is worth having early, before commitment to one approach drives all the other decisions.

Here's an honest assessment of each system's strengths and limitations in the context of commercial construction.

## Structural Steel

Steel's primary advantage is speed. Fabrication happens off-site while the foundation is being built, erection is fast, and the structure goes up quickly. Steel is also highly adaptable: span ranges are flexible, connections can be designed to accommodate almost any condition, and modifications during construction or future renovation are relatively straightforward.

The limitations are cost sensitivity to fabrication volume, fire protection requirements (typically spray-applied or intumescent coatings, which add cost), and the fact that steel erection requires a competent contractor. In markets where steel fabricators are backlogged, schedule advantages can disappear.

Steel is a strong choice for: irregular column grids, long spans, structures that need to be modified in the future, and projects where schedule is the primary driver.

## Cast-in-Place Concrete

Concrete offers mass, stiffness, and durability that the other systems don't match. A well-designed concrete frame performs exceptionally in seismic events, resists progressive collapse, and carries load through multiple redundant paths. Post-tensioned concrete in particular offers span efficiency that steel and timber can't always match at the same floor depth.

Concrete also integrates structure and fire resistance: a PT flat plate doesn't need additional fireproofing. And concrete buildings perform well acoustically, which matters in residential, hotel, and mixed-use occupancies.

The limitations are formwork cost, schedule (concrete gains strength over time and can't be stripped until it reaches adequate capacity), and the dependence on a skilled local concrete subcontractor base. Concrete also doesn't respond well to post-construction modifications. Cutting or coring a PT slab is a specialized operation.

Concrete is a strong choice for: mid-rise residential and hotel, parking structures, buildings in high-seismic zones requiring ductile performance, and projects where acoustic separation is a priority.

## Mass Timber

Mass timber (cross-laminated timber, glulam, and structural composite lumber) has gained significant traction in recent years, and for good reason. The aesthetic is genuinely appealing, the sustainability story is compelling for certain owners, and the prefabrication of mass timber components can offer schedule advantages similar to steel.

The structural limitations are real, however. Timber is combustible, which triggers code complexity in taller buildings (though IBC 2021 now allows mass timber up to 18 stories with specific conditions). Connections in mass timber require careful detailing. Wood is orthotropic and can split along the grain in ways steel and concrete don't. Moisture management during and after construction is critical. And the lateral system typically still relies on concrete shear walls or steel frames, meaning mass timber isn't usually a pure structural solution.

Mass timber is a strong choice for: low to mid-rise office, institutional, and multifamily projects where the exposed structure aesthetic adds value, owners with genuine sustainability commitments, and projects where the architect and SE have experience with the system.

## The Honest Comparison

No single material wins across all project types. The right answer depends on spans, height, seismic zone, local contractor capability, budget, and schedule. An SE who has designed in all three systems, not just one, can give an honest assessment rather than a default recommendation.

The best time to have this conversation is at schematic design, when the choice is still free.
