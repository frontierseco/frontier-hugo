Every contractor has a story. The structural drawings said one thing; the field said something else. The result was a change order, a schedule hit, and an uncomfortable conversation with the owner. Sometimes the fix was straightforward. Sometimes it wasn't.

Structural surprises during construction are not random. Most of them have identifiable causes: incomplete design information, inadequate site investigation, poor coordination, or changes that weren't fully engineered before they were built. Understanding those causes is the first step toward eliminating them.

## The Hidden Cost Multiplier

The direct cost of a structural change order (additional material, labor, engineering) is usually the smaller part of the impact. The larger costs are indirect:

**Schedule.** Work in progress stops or slows while the engineering response is developed. Trades that follow the affected work are displaced. A one-week structural RFI response can result in two weeks of downstream impact.

**Subcontractor disruption.** When work has to be redone, the affected sub is pulled back to a sequence they've already left. Remobilization is expensive. Work that was completed may need to be removed.

**Owner relationship.** Change orders of any kind require owner approval. A structural change order, especially one that results from an error or omission in the design documents, damages confidence. Owners remember these conversations at the end of the project and at the start of the next one.

**Contingency consumption.** Structural surprises in early construction phases consume the contingency that was held for later risk. When the contingency is gone early, the risk exposure for the remainder of the project is unmitigated.

## Common Sources of Structural Surprises

**Undocumented existing conditions.** On renovation and adaptive reuse projects, the as-built condition of the existing structure rarely matches the drawings, or there are no drawings. Steel members that were modified, reinforcing that was added or omitted, concrete that was poured under questionable conditions. These conditions don't reveal themselves until the contractor opens up the building. Exploratory demolition before design completion is one of the best investments on a renovation project.

**Inadequate geotechnical investigation.** Subsurface conditions that weren't investigated properly produce foundation surprises. Unexpected rock, soft pockets, groundwater at a level that changes the foundation type: all avoidable with a thorough soils investigation and not avoidable without one.

**Coordination failures.** Structural members that conflict with mechanical, electrical, or plumbing systems generate field changes. The beam is where the duct needs to run. The column is where the pipe goes. These conflicts are found during construction when coordination wasn't done during design.

**Scope changes that weren't re-engineered.** Owner changes during construction sometimes get implemented without a full engineering review. A wall that moves, a floor load that increases, an opening that gets added. Each of these can have structural consequences that aren't obvious without analysis.

## How to Reduce Structural Surprises

None of this is new information to experienced contractors. The mitigation strategies are also well understood:

- Invest in the site investigation before design starts
- Require exploratory demolition before structural redesign on renovation projects
- Use a BIM or coordination process that catches conflicts before construction
- Establish a clear process for engineering review of all scope changes, regardless of how minor they appear
- Select an SE who responds to RFIs quickly and accurately, and who has experience solving field problems

The goal isn't to eliminate all change. Construction is inherently dynamic. The goal is to ensure that the surprises that do occur are handled efficiently, with minimal impact to the project and the relationships that carry it.

The best structural engineering makes the contractor's job easier. That's not an accident. It's a design standard.
