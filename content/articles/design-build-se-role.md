Design-build changes the relationship between the structural engineer and the rest of the project team in ways that matter. In a traditional design-bid-build model, the SE produces documents, the GC prices and builds them, and the two parties interact primarily through RFIs and submittals. In design-build, that separation disappears, and the SE's ability to make fast, informed decisions with cost and schedule in mind becomes a competitive advantage.

## The SE as Cost Driver

In design-build, the GC carries cost risk. Every structural decision (system selection, member sizes, connection types, foundation type) affects the cost that the GC is responsible for. An SE who understands this can make design choices that are structurally sound and cost-effective, rather than simply code-compliant.

This isn't about cutting corners. It's about knowing that a post-tensioned flat plate is cheaper to build than a steel frame at certain spans, that drilled piers are more economical than grade beams in certain soil conditions, that simple shear connections are faster to fabricate than moment connections. These are judgment calls that require both structural knowledge and construction familiarity.

An SE who has spent time in the field reviewing work, solving problems, and understanding how things are actually built brings different value to a design-build team than one who has only worked from a desk. That experience shows up in documents that are easier to build and estimates that hold.

## Fast Decisions, Maintained Quality

Design-build schedules compress design phases. Owners want earlier starts, GCs want earlier pricing, and the design team is expected to provide enough information for both before the full design is complete. For the SE, this means producing reliable structural narratives, preliminary member sizes, and foundation recommendations early, before calculations are complete, with enough confidence to support a GC's estimate.

This requires judgment. An engineer who won't provide preliminary information without running full calculations slows the team down. One who provides information carelessly creates risk. The balance is in knowing what can be reasonably sized with experience and what genuinely requires analysis before a number is committed.

## Risk Allocation in Design-Build

In design-bid-build, design errors sit with the design team and construction errors sit with the contractor. Design-build blurs this line. When the GC and design team are on the same contract, risk is shared, which creates incentive for the SE to produce clear, buildable documents, and for the contractor to raise constructability concerns early rather than after the fact.

This shared incentive is one of design-build's genuine advantages. Problems that would generate adversarial change orders in traditional delivery get solved collaboratively in design-build, because both parties are exposed to the outcome.

## What Makes an SE a Good Design-Build Partner

The contractors who have worked in design-build long enough to have preferences will tell you: they want an SE who returns calls, gives real answers to preliminary questions, understands cost, and doesn't over-engineer. They want someone who has been on a job site and can visualize what they're drawing.

On the other side, the SE needs a GC who shares relevant cost data, involves the design team in constructability reviews, and gives early notice of field conditions that affect the design. The best design-build projects are ones where this exchange is natural and ongoing.

Design-build rewards structural engineers who are engaged business partners, not just technical producers. The firms that understand that distinction build long relationships with GCs who keep coming back.
