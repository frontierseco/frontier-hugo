If you have applied for a building permit in Colorado Springs or elsewhere in Colorado, you may have been told that you need a structural engineer letter. This article explains what a structural letter is, when building departments require one, what it contains, and how to get one.

## What Is a Structural Engineer Letter?

A structural engineer letter (sometimes called a structural report, structural assessment letter, or SE letter) is a written document signed and sealed by a licensed Professional Engineer. The letter conveys the engineer's professional opinion about a structural condition or design.

Unlike a full set of structural drawings, a letter does not include detailed framing plans or construction details. It conveys a conclusion and the technical basis for that conclusion. Building departments accept letters when the scope is limited enough that full drawings are not warranted, or when the letter supplements existing drawings.

---

## When Colorado Building Departments Require a Structural Letter

### Existing Structure Evaluation
When a permit application involves modifying or adding load to an existing structure — and the existing structure does not have original drawings — the building department may require an engineer's letter confirming the structure can handle the proposed change. Examples:
- Adding solar panels to an existing roof (weight and wind uplift on existing framing)
- Mounting mechanical equipment on a roof or wall
- Installing a heavy item (generator, hot tub, large HVAC unit) on an existing floor or deck

### Non-Standard Construction
Structures built with unusual materials, non-standard spans, or methods that don't follow standard prescriptive code paths often require engineer certification that the construction is structurally adequate.

### Unpermitted Work
Work done without a permit that is being legalized through a permit after the fact often requires an engineer's letter stating that the construction meets structural code requirements as installed.

### Post-Disaster Evaluation
After a hail storm, wind event, or other damaging weather event, insurance companies and sometimes building departments require an engineer letter documenting observed damage and its structural significance.

### Pre-Purchase or Lender Requirements
Lenders occasionally require a structural engineer letter as a condition of financing, particularly for properties with visible distress or unusual construction.

## What a Structural Letter Should Include

A letter that satisfies the Pikes Peak Regional Building Department and other Colorado jurisdictions typically includes:

1. **Engineer identification** — name, license number, and company
2. **Project identification** — address, owner name, and description of the scope
3. **Basis of evaluation** — site visit date, documents reviewed, and what was observed or analyzed
4. **Findings** — what the engineer found (conditions, measurements, analysis results)
5. **Conclusions** — the engineer's professional opinion on the structural adequacy of the subject matter
6. **Limitations** — what the letter does and does not cover (important for honest letters)
7. **Stamp and signature** — the engineer's Colorado PE seal

A letter without a site visit that relies only on photos or descriptions is less defensible and may not satisfy the building department. Ask the engineer what their evaluation basis will be.

## Structural Letters vs. Full Structural Drawings

| | Structural Letter | Full Structural Drawings |
|---|---|---|
| **Use** | Limited scope evaluations, existing structure certification | New construction, additions, significant structural modifications |
| **Contents** | Written findings and conclusions | Plans, sections, details, specifications |
| **Permit use** | Supplements application or standalone for minor scopes | Primary structural submittal |
| **Cost** | Lower | Higher |
| **Site visit** | Often required | Usually required |

The building department determines which is required. If your permit application has been returned asking for "structural engineer documentation," confirm with the plan check whether a letter is sufficient or whether full drawings are required.

## Common Questions

**Can I get a letter without a site visit?**
Sometimes. For simple scope items where photos and dimensions are unambiguous, a site visit may not be required. For anything involving existing conditions that are not fully documented, a site visit produces a better and more defensible letter.

**Will the letter guarantee permit approval?**
No. The engineer can only attest to what was evaluated. The building department makes the final determination on permit approval. If the building department has questions, we respond to plan check comments at no additional charge for minor clarifications.

**What if the structure is not adequate?**
If our evaluation reveals that the existing structure does not meet code requirements or cannot support the proposed modification, we tell you — and we discuss options for remediation or redesign.
