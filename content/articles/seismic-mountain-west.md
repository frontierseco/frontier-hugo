Seismic design is sometimes treated as a California problem — something that shows up on the West Coast and doesn't apply elsewhere. That misunderstanding creates real risk on Colorado projects, and it leaves some California project teams relying on rules of thumb that don't reflect the complexity of high-seismic design.

The reality is that seismic design drives fundamental decisions about structural systems, connections, and detailing — and those decisions need to be made early.

## Colorado's Seismic Reality

Colorado sits in a moderate seismic zone. The Front Range in particular has active fault systems, and the soil conditions common to the region — expansive clays, loose alluvials — can amplify ground motion significantly. ASCE 7 and the IBC require seismic analysis based on mapped spectral acceleration values and site class, and in many Colorado locations those requirements produce designs that look similar to parts of California.

Projects that assume Colorado is low-seismic and then discover the actual site class and Seismic Design Category late in design face change orders. The right time to run the numbers is before the structural system is selected.

## What Seismic Design Actually Controls

Seismic design isn't just about making things stronger. The governing concern is ductility — the ability of a structure to absorb and dissipate energy during an earthquake without collapsing. That means:

**System selection matters.** Not all structural systems perform the same way in seismic events. Special moment frames, special concentrically braced frames, and special shear walls provide the ductility required in high-seismic zones. Ordinary systems may not be permitted, or may require significant overstrength to qualify.

**Detailing is everything.** A special moment frame is only special if it's detailed to the AISC seismic provisions — specific connection geometries, weld procedures, protected zones. The same applies to concrete shear walls and special reinforced masonry. An experienced SE knows the difference between a compliant and a non-compliant detail.

**Diaphragms and collectors.** The floor and roof diaphragms are what transfer seismic forces to the vertical lateral system. Collector elements — the members that gather diaphragm forces and deliver them to shear walls or frames — are frequently undersized on projects where seismic wasn't taken seriously at the start.

## California Projects: Not All Zones Are Equal

California's seismic hazard varies considerably. A project in the Central Valley doesn't face the same demands as one in the Los Angeles basin or along the Hayward Fault. Site-specific ground motion studies are sometimes warranted for critical facilities or unusual soil conditions. The code floor is a starting point, not always the end of the conversation.

## What This Means for Architects and Contractors

Seismic requirements affect where shear walls and braced frames can be located, which directly affects floor plan flexibility. An architect who understands the lateral system early can work with the SE to locate resistance elements where they make architectural sense. One who finds out late has to accommodate walls and frames that are structurally correct but architecturally awkward.

For contractors, seismic detailing — moment connection welding, special inspection requirements, reinforcing congestion in shear walls — affects schedule and cost. These aren't surprises if the system is understood early. They are surprises when they're discovered in the field.

The Mountain West demands structural engineers who know seismic design from practice, not just from the code book. The difference shows up in the details.
